About Adaeze

Adaeze Okonkwo was born in Enugu, Nigeria, the third daughter of a schoolteacher father who read Achebe aloud at dinner and a mother who carried the old stories in Igbo that no one had thought to write down. She grew up between two languages and two worlds — and has spent her writing life in that threshold space.

Her debut novel When the Harmattan Speaks (2019) was longlisted for the Booker Prize and translated into 23 languages. Her second, The Weight of Red Clay (2022), won the Africa Prize for Literature and was described by The Guardian as "a book that bends reality until reality admits the truth."

She writes about women who refuse the stories they were handed. About grief that wears the face of a river. About what happens when a culture's silence finally breaks open.

Chapter 12: The River Remembers
Draft · Chapter 12 · 2,847 words written today
The river had a name before the missionaries came. Before the maps were drawn with their hard European lines that cut through the living body of the land like a blade through something that did not know it could bleed. Mama called it Oji Mmiri — the water that carries memory — and she said this in Igbo, always in Igbo, because some things lose themselves entirely when you put them in English.

Nkechi stood at the bank where her grandmother had stood, where her grandmother's grandmother had stood, her feet in the red clay that stained everything it touched. The city was behind her. The river was in front. Between them: everything that mattered.

She had come back to bury her mother. She had not expected to find her.
