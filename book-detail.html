About this book

The year is 1969. In the state of Kerala, on the southernmost tip of India, a sybaritic englishman is found drowned. His death will unravel a family — and expose the ancient, arbitrary Love Laws: who should be loved, and how, and how much.

Told through the eyes of fraternal twins Rahel and Estha, Roy's debut novel moves across time — from the luminous world of their childhood to the wreckage of their adulthood — in a story where a single terrible afternoon casts a shadow across an entire life.

Roy writes in a language that has never existed before — Malayalam rhythms poured into English syntax, caste and history and nature braided into sentences that bloom and break apart like the Kerala monsoon. The God of Small Things is one of the few novels that has genuinely changed how English fiction sounds.

Chapter 1 · Paradise Pickles & Preserves

May in Ayemenem is a hot, brooding month. The days are long and humid. The river shrinks and the sand gets pale and silty. Cattle egrets stand in the middle of fields and cattle browse the shallow water.

Afternoons are long in Ayemenem. Too hot for any thing to move. The world is lulled into a sort of lazy, heavy silence. Even the butterflies move slowly, like a thought.

The nights are lit by fireflies. Ancient rhythms and older patterns. The earth keeps its own time. Kerala does not hurry. It never has.

In the house of Shoba and Chacko, the twins Rahel and Estha arrived in their mother's womb — together, and yet not together. They grew in their separate silences, wrapped around each other like question marks.

The History House stood across the river. Nobody spoke of what had happened there. The Love Laws had been obeyed, and the thing that needed to be forgotten had been forgotten. Or tried to be.

Estha was Ofeelus. Rahel was Rahel. These were not diminutive names, not pet names. They were the real names, and they contained within them the whole architecture of what the two of them were to each other — which was: everything.
